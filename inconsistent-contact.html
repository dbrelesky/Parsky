Your Diagnosis

The Problem

Inconsistent Contact, Block-Right & the Occasional Shank

You're new to the game and your strike is all over the face - some thin, some fat, some off the toe. Your stock miss flies hard to the right (a block or slice), and every so often a shot rockets dead right off the hosel: the shank. The shots feel random, but they're not. Two linked faults are driving almost all of it.

Root Cause

No Low-Point Control + an Open Face from Too Close to the Ball

Two things are happening, and they feed each other:

  • Inconsistent contact: The bottom of your swing (your "low point") moves around. When it bottoms out behind the ball you hit it fat; when you pull up to save it you hit it thin. You haven't yet trained the club to brush the ground after the ball.
  • Block-right: The clubface is open at impact - usually a weak grip plus a body that stalls instead of rotating, so the face never squares up. The ball starts right and stays right (or curves further right as a slice).
  • The shank: Contact off the hosel - the rounded neck where the shaft meets the head. It happens when the club moves out and away from you through impact, almost always because your weight drifts onto your toes and/or you stand too close. It is a contact error, not a "swing disaster." It is very fixable.

Good news: as a newer player you have no bad habits burned in. Build the right setup and one good contact pattern now and you skip years of fighting these misses.

Why This Happens

Three setup & motion contributors typically drive this pattern:

1. Setup that points you right: A weak grip (hands turned too far left on the handle), an open clubface at address, and aim that's off all push the ball right before you even swing.

2. Weight to the toes / standing tall through impact: When your balance falls toward the ball, the hosel finds it - that's the shank - and your low point becomes unpredictable.

3. Hands and arms instead of the body: A new golfer's instinct is to hit at the ball with the arms while the body stops turning. That stalls the face open and makes the strike a guess.

The Fix - Overview

Build the Setup, Then Train One Clean Strike

You are NOT rebuilding a swing - you're building one for the first time, the simple way. The plan is to:

  1. Fix your foundation - grip, aim, posture and ball position - so the ball stops starting right
  2. Kill the shank by keeping your weight in your heels and your balance away from the ball through impact
  3. Control your low point so the club brushes the turf just after the ball, every time
  4. Square the face with body rotation - a slightly stronger grip plus a turn-through, so your stock shot starts straight

The 30-Day Plan

3-4 days per week, 30-60 minutes per session. Each day is designed to be done at the range or at home (marked accordingly).

Week 1 - Setup & Centered Contact

Before you change anything about your swing, we build the foundation: grip, aim, posture, ball position, and a first feel for where the club bottoms out. Get these right and most of the block-right and the shanks fade on their own. No full-speed swings this week - slow, controlled reps.

Day 1 - Diagnosis & Baseline

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: See your real pattern - both where the ball goes and where it's hitting the face.

1
Film your swing (see Film Your Swing section). Capture 5 swings face-on and 5 down-the-line with a 7-iron.
2
Foot-spray the face: Mist the clubface with athlete's foot spray (or use impact tape). Hit 10 balls, checking the strike mark after each. Marks toward the hosel (the neck) are your shank warning; marks scattered toe-to-heel mean no center control yet. This is your contact baseline.
3
Hit 20 balls with a 7-iron at a target. After each, note two things: direction (left / straight / right / shank) and contact (fat / thin / flush). Tally them up - this is your Week 1 baseline.

Day 2 - Grip & Aim Reset

30 min | Range or Home ▼

Goal: Stop the ball starting right before you even swing. A weak grip and aiming right are the two biggest reasons new golfers block it.

1
Build a neutral-to-slightly-strong grip (15 min): Place the lead hand so you can see 2-3 knuckles looking down. The "V" between thumb and forefinger on both hands should point at your trail shoulder. A weak grip (hands turned too far left) is the #1 reason new golfers leave the face open and block it right. Re-grip from scratch and make 30 slow rehearsal swings.
2
Railroad-track aim (10 min): Lay one alignment stick on the ground along your toes, pointing parallel-left of the target (like train tracks - the ball runs on the far rail). New golfers usually aim well right and then swing across it. Hit 15 balls checking feet, hips and shoulders all match the stick.
3
Mirror check (home option): Rehearse the new grip in a mirror until you can build it the same way every time without looking.

Day 3 - Posture & Ball Position

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Set a balanced posture and ball position that supports center contact - and keeps the hosel away from the ball.

1
Athletic posture (10 min): Tilt forward from the hips (not the waist), soft knees, let the arms hang under your shoulders. Set your weight in the middle of your feet, leaning very slightly toward the heels - never the toes. Falling toward your toes through the swing is what drags the hosel into the ball. Rehearse 15 setups.
2
Hand-width gap (10 min): At address there should be about a hand-width between the grip end and your belt buckle. Stand any closer and the shank is waiting. Hit 15 balls checking that gap on every setup.
3
Ball position (15 min): Play the ball in the middle of your stance for the 7-iron (it creeps forward toward the lead heel for longer clubs, back toward center for wedges). Hit 20 balls and note whether contact gets more consistent with a reliable ball position.

Day 4 - Find the Bottom (Low Point)

30 min | Range or Home ▼

Goal: Train the club to brush the ground after the ball. This is the single skill behind consistent contact.

1
Towel-behind-the-ball drill (15 min): Lay a small towel about one clubhead-width behind the ball. Hit 20 half-swing 7-irons trying to miss the towel and catch the ball first. If you keep hitting the towel, your low point is behind the ball - feel a little more weight on your lead foot at impact.
2
Line drill (10 min): Draw a line in the grass (or lay a tee) and make practice swings where the brush/divot starts just in front of the line, never behind it. 15 rehearsals, then 5 balls.
3
Lead-knee feel (5 min): To start the downswing, feel your lead knee move a touch toward the target. This simple move shifts your weight - and your low point - forward, ahead of the ball. No need to think about anything else.

Week 2 - Kill the Shank & Center the Strike

This is shank week. We attack the hosel directly with toe-strike drills, lock your balance into your heels, and start finding the center of the face on demand. Stay at half and three-quarter swings - speed comes later, contact comes first.

Day 5 - Kill the Shank

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Move contact off the hosel toward the center of the face. The shank is a contact error, not a curse - this drill fixes it fast.

1
Warm up (10 min): Towel-behind-the-ball drill (10 balls), checking your balance is in your heels, not your toes.
2
Toe-strike / two-ball drill (20 min): Place a second ball (or a tee) just outside the toe of your 7-iron, about an inch beyond your ball. Your job is to hit your ball and miss the outside one - which forces contact toward the toe and away from the hosel. Start with half swings. Shanks come from the club moving out toward the ball through impact; this drill trains the opposite. 25 balls.
3
Foot-spray confirm (15 min): Re-spray the face. Hit 15 balls and watch the mark migrate toward center. If you can string together 5 in a row with no hosel contact, the shank pattern is breaking.

Day 6 - Weight in the Heels (Balance)

30 min | Home or Range ▼

Goal: Stop your balance from falling toward the ball. When weight drifts onto the toes, the hosel finds the ball - this is the root of the shank.

1
Board / club-under-toes drill (10 min): Lay a 2x4 (or an alignment stick) on the ground under your toes. Make slow swings keeping pressure off the board - you can only do that by keeping your weight back toward your heels. 20 rehearsals. You should feel like you're sitting into your heels through impact.
2
Feet-together balance drill (10 min): Stand with your feet almost touching and hit 15 short shots (50-70 yards) with a 7-iron. With this base you simply can't lunge at the ball - it forces you to stay centered and rotate. A great anti-shank reset any time the hosel reappears.
3
Finish in balance (10 min): Hit 10 normal-stance balls and hold your finish for 3 seconds each, weight on your lead heel, chest to the target. If you fall forward onto your toes, that's your shank warning.

Day 7 - Center-Face Striking

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Find the middle of the face on demand. Center contact is more distance, more control, and the end of the random misses.

1
Warm up (10 min): Board-under-toes rehearsals (10), then towel-behind-ball (5 balls).
2
Tee gate around the ball (20 min): Stick two tees in the ground just wider than your clubhead - one a hair outside the toe, one a hair outside the heel - with the ball in the middle. Hit 25 balls (7-iron, 3/4 swing) trying to bring the club through the gate without clipping either tee. This narrows your strike to the center. If you keep nicking the inside tee, that's the hosel - revisit the toe drill from Day 5.
3
Compression check (15 min): Hit 15 balls and listen. Center strikes sound and feel different - a click, not a clack. Foot-spray every 5 balls to confirm the marks are clustering in the middle.

Day 8 - Half-Swing Flush Striking

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Lock in clean contact with controlled, repeatable 9-to-3 swings before you add full speed.

1
9-to-3 drill (20 min): Make swings where your lead arm goes from 9 o'clock (parallel, hip height) back to 3 o'clock through impact - no further. Hit 30 balls with a 7-iron. Short swings remove the lunge and let you feel ball-first, center-face contact. This is the foundation of every consistent striker.
2
Contact ladder (15 min): Hit 3 balls each with PW, 9i, 8i, 7i, 6i using the 9-to-3 feel. Keep the strike clean before you worry about distance. Note which club first loses center contact - that's your edge to keep working.
3
Compare to baseline (10 min): Foot-spray and hit 10 full-length 7-irons. Tally direction and contact, and compare to your Day 1 numbers.
Benchmark

By the end of Week 2 you should have zero or one shank per 20 balls (down from several) and most strikes landing in the middle third of the face. If the hosel is still showing up often, repeat Days 5-6 before moving on - the rest of the plan builds on clean contact.

Week 3 - Square the Face & Stop the Block-Right

Your contact is reliable now, so we turn to direction. This week squares the clubface with a better grip and a real release, neutralizes the over-the-top path, then takes the new strike through the whole bag. The ball should start straightening out.

Day 9 - Stronger Grip & Release

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Square the face so the ball stops starting right. Most of the block comes from an open face and a body that quits turning.

1
Warm up (10 min): 9-to-3 swings (10 balls) to re-find center contact.
2
Release / toe-up to toe-up (20 min): Make 9-to-3 swings where the clubface toe points up at hip height on the way back AND on the way through - that's the face rotating square instead of staying open. Feel the trail forearm cross over the lead through impact. Hit 25 balls. The ball should start much straighter. If it now draws a touch, perfect.
3
Rotate, don't stall (15 min): Hit 15 balls feeling your chest and belt buckle keep turning to face the target after impact. A stalled body forces the hands to flip or leaves the face open - keep turning through. Tally how many start straight or left vs. right.

Day 10 - Fix the Path (Stop Going Over the Top)

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Stop swinging across the ball (out-to-in), the other half of the slice and a frequent shank cause.

1
Towel-under-the-arms drill (10 min): Tuck a towel (or headcover) under both armpits and make slow swings without dropping it. This keeps your arms connected to your body so they stop throwing out and over the top. 15 rehearsals, then 5 balls.
2
Path gate (15 min): Set a headcover (or a tee) just outside and behind the ball, on the target line. To miss it, the club has to approach slightly from the inside instead of over the top. Hit 20 balls (7-iron) feeling the club drop "behind you" a touch in the downswing, then swing out toward the target.
3
Start-line check (20 min): Hit 20 balls and watch where each one starts. Over-the-top slices start left then peel right; a neutral strike starts on line. You want the start line straight and the curve gone. Foot-spray occasionally to make sure the better path is still finding the center of the face.

Day 11 - Full-Bag Contact Ladder

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Carry clean, straight contact into every club, including the driver - which exposes any leftover face or path error.

1
Warm up (10 min): 9-to-3 release swings (10 balls).
2
Ascending ladder (20 min): Hit 5 balls each: PW, 9i, 8i, 7i, 6i, hybrid, driver. Same grip, same setup gap, weight in the heels. Note the first club where center contact or start line breaks down - longer clubs are harder, so that's where to keep working.
3
Driver off a tee (15 min): Tee it up and hit 15 drivers at 80% speed. Aim down the middle and let the ball start straight. A straight 190 in play beats a 230 slice into the trees. Don't chase distance yet - reward yourself for center contact and a straight start.

Day 12 - Pressure Contact Game

30 min | Range ▼

Goal: Prove the new contact holds when a shot "counts." Changes that work mindlessly but collapse under attention aren't trained yet.

1
Par-18 contact game (20 min): Play 9 "holes." Each hole: pick a club and target, full routine, hit ONE ball. Score par if the strike is center and the start line is straight; bogey if it's off-center but playable; double if it's a shank or hard block. No re-hits. Add up your score and beat it next time.
2
Beat-your-best (10 min): Foot-spray and hit 10 balls. Count center strikes. Write the number down - this is the score you chase every session.
Benchmark

By the end of Week 3 you should be striking the middle of the face most of the time, shanks should be rare, and your stock ball flight should start roughly straight instead of leaking right. If the block is still your main miss, give the grip and release from Day 9 another session before Week 4.

Week 4 - Take It to the Course

The contact and direction work is done. This week builds a simple pre-shot routine, sharpens your scoring clubs and chipping, and gets you playing real shots with smart, beginner-friendly course management.

Day 13 - Pre-Shot Routine & One Swing Thought

30 min | Range ▼

Goal: Build a simple, repeatable routine so you stop thinking about mechanics over the ball.

1
Design your routine (10 min): Stand behind the ball. Pick a target. Take one practice swing with your ONE swing thought (pick whichever helps most: "weight in the heels," "release the face," or "turn through"). Step in, set your grip and the hand-width gap, one look at the target, swing. The whole thing should take 15-20 seconds. Rehearse it 10 times without a ball.
2
Routine + ball (20 min): Hit 30 balls and give every single one the full routine. No rapid-fire range balls - that's how the old random contact creeps back. If you skip the routine, step off and restart.
One Swing Thought Maximum

On the course you get ONE swing thought, never two or three. Pick the cue that's giving you the most center strikes and the straightest start, and trust it. Every other drill stays on the range.

Day 14 - Scoring Clubs & Chipping

45 min | Range & Short-Game Area ▼

Goal: Sharpen the clubs that save the most strokes for a new golfer - wedges and a simple, repeatable chip. Good chipping covers a lot of missed greens.

1
Wedge distance control (15 min): With your PW, hit 5 balls each at full, 3/4, and 1/2 swings. Note roughly how far each carries. Knowing three wedge distances is worth more to your score than 10 extra yards off the tee.
2
Basic chip (15 min): Narrow stance, weight favoring the lead foot, ball back of center, hands ahead. Make a small putting-style stroke and let the loft do the work - no scooping (scooping is where chip-shanks and thins come from). Chip 20 balls to a target, landing them on a spot and letting them roll.
3
Up-and-down game (15 min): Drop 10 balls around a green. For each: chip on, then putt out. Count how many you get down in 2. This is the skill that turns blow-up holes into bogeys.

Day 15 - Full Simulation Round

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Play a simulated 18 holes on the range - your final dress rehearsal before the course.

1
18-hole simulation (40 min): Pick a layout from memory. Each hole: choose a club off the tee, hit it; based on the result, choose an approach club, hit it. Full routine on every shot. Score generously (center contact in play = good drive; on the "green" = good approach). Write your score.
2
Review (5 min): Count your misses. How many shanks? How many blocks-right? How many clean, straight shots? Compare to Day 1 - this is your 30-day progress report.

Day 16 - Your First Smart Round

Round Day ▼

Goal: Play a relaxed round with ONE swing thought, your routine, and beginner-smart course management. This is validation day.

1
Pre-round warm-up (15 min): Towel-behind-ball (10 balls), 9-to-3 strikes (10 balls), 5 tee shots. No more - save the good feels for the course.
2
On-course rules:
  • ONE swing thought only - the one from Day 13
  • Full pre-shot routine on every full swing, including the gap check that keeps the hosel away
  • Aim for the middle of the fairway and the middle of the green - never flags or gaps
  • Take an extra club and swing easy; a smooth 80% swing strikes the center far more often
  • If you're in trouble, just punch back to the fairway - turn doubles into bogeys
  • Track: shanks, blocks-right, fairways hit, and up-and-downs
3
Post-round review: Write down your score, number of shanks/blocks, and how the chipping held up. This is your course baseline - and the start of your next 30 days.

Expectation Setting

Your first rounds won't be flawless, and that's fine - you're brand new. Some old misses will visit under pressure. Trust the range work, keep the one swing thought, and play for bogey. Avoiding the big number is how new golfers break 100, then 90. The pure strikes will come more and more often.

Drill Library

Quick reference for every drill in the plan. Bookmark this section for range sessions.

Awareness

Foot-Spray Face Check

What it fixes: Removes the guesswork - shows you exactly where on the face you're striking the ball.

Setup: Mist the clubface with athlete's foot spray (or use impact/dr-shot tape). 7-iron.

Execution: Hit a ball and read the mark. Toward the hosel (neck) = shank danger. Toe or heel = no center control. Middle = pure. Re-spray every 5 balls and watch the marks cluster toward the center as you work.

Reps: Use throughout every session as your feedback loop.

Anti-Shank

Toe-Strike / Two-Ball Drill

What it fixes: The shank - contact off the hosel.

Setup: Place a second ball (or a tee) just outside the toe of the club, about an inch beyond your ball. 7-iron.

Execution: Hit your ball and miss the outside one. This forces contact toward the toe, the opposite of the hosel. The shank comes from the club moving out toward the ball through impact; this trains it to stay in. Start with half swings.

Reps: 25 balls. The #1 drill any time the shank reappears.

Balance

Board-Under-Toes Balance Drill

What it fixes: Weight falling onto the toes - the root cause of the shank and wandering contact.

Setup: Lay a 2x4 or an alignment stick on the ground under your toes.

Execution: Make swings keeping pressure off the board, which is only possible if you keep your weight back toward your heels. Feel like you "sit into" your heels through impact.

Reps: 20 slow rehearsals; can be done at home without hitting balls.

Contact

Towel-Behind-the-Ball Drill

What it fixes: Low-point control - hitting the ground behind the ball (fat) or pulling up off it (thin).

Setup: Lay a small towel about one clubhead-width behind the ball. 7-iron, half swing.

Execution: Strike the ball first and miss the towel. If you keep hitting the towel, your low point is behind the ball - feel a little more weight on your lead foot at impact and let your lead knee move toward the target.

Reps: 20 balls. A staple warm-up for every session.

Center Contact

Tee Gate Around the Ball

What it fixes: Toe-and-heel scatter; narrows your strike to the center of the face.

Setup: Stick two tees in the ground just wider than your clubhead - one a hair outside the toe, one a hair outside the heel - ball in the middle.

Execution: Swing the club through the gate without clipping either tee. Nicking the inside (heel) tee means the hosel is creeping in - revisit the toe drill. Start with 3/4 swings.

Reps: 25 balls with a 7-iron.

Contact

9-to-3 Half-Swing Drill

What it fixes: Random contact from an over-long, lunging swing. Builds the repeatable strike everything else is built on.

Setup: 7-iron, normal address.

Execution: Swing your lead arm from 9 o'clock (parallel, hip height) back through to 3 o'clock - no further. Short and controlled so you can feel ball-first, center-face contact. Add length only once this is automatic.

Reps: 30 balls. The foundation drill of the whole plan.

Face

Toe-Up to Toe-Up Release

What it fixes: An open face at impact - the block-right and the slice.

Setup: Neutral-to-slightly-strong grip (2-3 knuckles visible on the lead hand). 7-iron, 9-to-3 swing.

Execution: The toe of the club should point up at hip height on the way back AND on the way through - that's the face rotating square instead of holding open. Feel the trail forearm cross over the lead through impact.

Reps: 25 balls; the ball should start straighter immediately.

Path

Towel-Under-Arms Connection

What it fixes: Throwing the arms out and over the top (the slicer's path).

Setup: Tuck a towel or headcover under both armpits.

Execution: Make slow swings without dropping it. This keeps the arms connected to your turning body so they stop casting out and across the ball. If the towel falls, your arms separated.

Reps: 15 rehearsals, then 10 balls at half speed.

Film Your Swing

Getting swing video is the single most valuable thing you can do without a launch monitor. Here's exactly how to set it up.

📱➡️

Down-the-Line (DTL)

  • Stand directly behind the ball, on the target line extended
  • Camera at hand height (not ground level, not head height)
  • Distance: far enough to see the full swing arc with the club visible at the top of the backswing
  • Aim camera at your hands at address
  • Use slow-motion (240fps on iPhone) if available

What it shows: Swing path (over the top vs. inside), how close you stand to the ball, whether the club moves out toward the ball through impact (the shank), early extension

📱⬆️

Face-On (FO)

  • Stand directly in front of the golfer, perpendicular to the target line
  • Camera at belt/hip height
  • Center the frame on the ball position
  • Distance: full body in frame with room above for the club at the top
  • Use slow-motion if available

What it shows: Balance (toes vs. heels), weight shift, ball position, low point (where the divot starts), face release, finish balance

Filming Tips

Use a tripod or phone mount - hand-held video is nearly useless for swing analysis.

Film 5 swings from each angle with the same club (7-iron is ideal).

Use slow-motion mode - 240fps on iPhone (Settings → Camera → Record Slo-mo). This lets you scrub through impact frame-by-frame.

Film in good light - avoid backlighting (don't face the sun).

Include the ball flight - let the camera run until the ball lands. Ball flight tells the story.

Don't "perform" - hit normal shots. The point is to capture your real swing, not your best swing.

What to Look For

When you review your video, check these specific things:

  • DTL - Distance to the ball: Is there about a hand-width between the grip end and your body at address? Standing too close invites the hosel.
  • DTL - Downswing path: Do your arms throw out and over the top (across the ball), or does the club drop slightly inside? Over the top causes both the slice and many shanks.
  • DTL - Impact balance: Has your weight fallen toward your toes / the ball? It should stay back toward your heels. Toe-falling = shanks.
  • FO - Low point: Where does the divot/brush start? It should be just after the ball, not behind it.
  • FO - Face & release: Is the clubface squaring up (toe rotating over) through impact, or staying open and pointing right? An open face is your block.
  • FO - Finish: Can you hold a balanced finish with your weight on your lead heel and chest to the target?

Course Strategy for New Golfers

Beyond ball striking - how to actually shoot lower scores while you're still learning.

The Math

Breaking 100, Then 90 - What It Takes

You do not need pars to break 100. The math is friendlier than you think:

  • Break 100: nine bogeys and nine double-bogeys = 99. That's it.
  • Break 90: mostly bogeys with a handful of pars.
  • Avoid the blow-up hole. New golfers don't lose strokes on bad swings - they lose them on triple-and-worse holes: a lost ball, a shank into trouble, three putts.
  • Two fewer disasters per round is often the difference between 104 and 98.

Tee Shots - Keep It In Play

  • Play the club you can find. If the driver is still wild, hit a hybrid or even a 7-iron off the tee. A ball in play beats 40 extra yards in the trees every time.
  • Aim away from your miss. While the block-right is still around, aim at the left half of the fairway so your miss finishes in the short grass instead of out of bounds.
  • Swing at 80%. A smooth, balanced swing finds the center of the face far more often than a full-power lunge - and balance is your anti-shank insurance.

Approach & Around the Green - Aim Big

  • Aim at the middle of the green, never the flag. The center is the biggest target and keeps you away from bunkers and water.
  • Take more club and swing easy. Most beginners come up short. One more club with a smooth swing beats a max-effort short club.
  • Lay up to a full wedge. If you can't reach in two, leave yourself a comfortable distance instead of a fiddly half-shot from the rough.
  • Chip, don't flop. Around the green, use a simple low chip (Day 14) and just get it on the putting surface. Two putts from anywhere on the green is a great result.

The "Bogey is a Win" Mindset

  • Bogey is a great score when you're learning. Stop trying to make par from trouble - that's how doubles become triples.
  • After a bad shot, take your medicine. Punch back to the fairway, wedge on, two-putt. Move on.
  • One swing thought, one target, commit. Indecision over the ball brings back the shank and the block. Pick your target, trust your routine, and swing.
  • Have fun. You're new - every clean strike is progress. Celebrate the good ones and shrug off the rest.

Recommended Training Aids

  • Athlete's foot spray or impact tape (essential): A few dollars. Sprayed on the face, it shows exactly where you're striking the ball - your shank early-warning system.
  • Alignment sticks (essential): $10-15. Two minimum. Used for aim, the tee/path gate, and balance (under the toes). Non-negotiable.
  • Phone tripod/mount (essential): $15-20. For filming your swing. A suction-cup mount on a range ball basket works too.
  • Tour Striker Smart Ball ($30): Inflatable ball held between the forearms - keeps the arms connected so they stop throwing over the top. Good for home reps.
  • Basic launch monitor (optional): A Garmin R10 (~$400) gives real face and path numbers. Not required at all as a beginner, but it speeds up the feedback loop later.
