Your Diagnosis

The Problem

The Two-Way Miss: Push-Right & Pull-Hook

Out of 100 balls, you're hitting roughly 40 push-right (or push-fade), 40 pull-hooks, and 20 straight. Your good shot is a draw. This is one of the most common patterns for skilled golfers — and it has a clear mechanical cause.

Root Cause

Excessively In-to-Out Path + Timing-Dependent Face

Your swing path is pointing too far right of your target (likely +4 to +8 degrees in-to-out). From that exaggerated inside path, two things happen:

  • Push-right: The clubface is open to the target but roughly square to your (too-inside) path. Ball starts right and stays right.
  • Pull-hook: Your hands instinctively flip to close the face. When they over-rotate, the face snaps closed relative to the path. Ball starts left and curves more left.
  • Good draw: The flip timing happens to be perfect — face is slightly closed to path, slightly open to target. This is a 20% window.

The core issue is not your hands. It's that your path is so far inside that the margin for correct face angle is impossibly small. You're playing a timing game you can't win consistently.

Why This Happens

Three mechanical contributors typically drive this pattern:

1. Getting "stuck": The club drops too far behind your body in the downswing. Your hips outrace your arms, and your hands have to rescue the club through impact with a flip.

2. Early extension: Your hips thrust toward the ball instead of rotating. This traps the arms and forces the torso to stand up through impact.

3. Inside takeaway: The club drags too far inside in the first 12 inches, setting up the stuck position from the start.

The Fix — Overview

Moderate the Path, Train the Rotation

You do NOT need to rebuild your swing. Your natural draw tendency is an asset. The plan is to:

  1. Neutralize your path from +6 degrees to +1 to +3 degrees — still a draw, but with a much wider margin for face error
  2. Train continuous body rotation through impact so the face squares naturally without a hand flip
  3. Fix the takeaway so the club doesn't get trapped behind you in the first place
  4. Build a stock 3-5 yard draw you can repeat under pressure

The 30-Day Plan

3-4 days per week, 30-60 minutes per session. Each day is designed to be done at the range or at home (marked accordingly).

Day 2 — Takeaway Reset

30 min | Range or Home ▼

Goal: Fix the first 18 inches of your swing. Stop the club from dragging inside.

1
Takeaway checkpoint drill (15 min): With a 7-iron, make slow takeaways stopping when the shaft is parallel to the ground. Check: the clubhead should be outside your hands, toe pointing up. If the clubhead is behind your hands, you're too inside. Repeat 30 times slowly.
2
Width drill — Chris Mayson's key concept (10 min): Feel like you're pushing the grip end away from your body in the takeaway. The lead arm should stay extended, creating a wide arc. Think: "Reach away, not around." Make half-swings only, focusing on this wider takeaway.
3
If at the range: Hit 20 balls with this wider takeaway feel. Use 3/4 swings only, 7-iron. Don't worry about where the ball goes — just groove the new takeaway.

Day 3 — Path Awareness

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Feel the difference between your current (too inside) path and a more neutral path.

1
Alignment stick gate drill (15 min): Place two alignment sticks in the ground about 8 inches apart, angled so they create a gate just outside the ball. Your clubhead must pass through the gate into the ball. If you're swinging too far from the inside, you'll hit the outer stick. Start with slow half-swings, then gradually increase speed. 7-iron.
2
"Swing left" feel (15 min): This will feel completely wrong at first. Set up normally, then feel like you're swinging the club out to left field (for a righty). You're not actually swinging over the top — you're just counteracting your deeply ingrained inside path. Hit 20 balls with a 7-iron trying to hit gentle fades. If you can hit a fade, your path is getting closer to neutral.
3
Alternate: draw-fade-draw-fade (15 min): Hit 10 balls alternating intentional draws and fades. The goal isn't shot shape perfection — it's proving you can influence path direction. Most two-way-miss golfers cannot intentionally hit a fade. Learning to do so is the cure.

Day 4 — Feet Together + Rotation

30 min | Range ▼

Goal: Feel proper body rotation without sway or early extension.

1
Feet-together drill (15 min): Put your feet completely together. Hit 25 balls with a 7-iron, 3/4 swing. This drill makes it physically impossible to sway, slide, or get stuck. If you can hit it solid with feet together, your rotation is working. Note: you will lose distance. That's fine — you're training sequencing.
2
Rotation checkpoint (5 min): After the feet-together balls, return to normal stance. Make slow-motion swings and stop at impact. Check: is your belt buckle turning toward the target? Is your chest rotating through? If your body stalls and your hands flip, that's the hook factory. You want continuous rotation.
3
Full swings with new feels (10 min): Hit 15 balls at 80% speed combining: wider takeaway + rotation through impact. Tally your results vs. Day 1 baseline.

Week 2 — Path Correction & Body Training

You've built awareness. Now we ingrain the neutral path and proper rotation with higher-intensity drills. This is the hardest week — the new feels will be uncomfortable. Trust the process.

Day 5 — The Fade Challenge

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Intentionally hit fades to retrain your path toward neutral. If you can hit a controlled fade, you've broken the in-to-out stranglehold.

1
Warm up (10 min): Takeaway checkpoint drill (10 reps). Then feet-together drill (10 balls).
2
Intentional fade practice (20 min): Open your stance slightly (feet pointing ~10 yards left of target). Swing along your foot line. Feel like the club exits left after impact. Use 7-iron. Hit 30 balls trying to start the ball straight or slightly left and fade it gently right. Don't worry if they go dead straight — that's actually perfect. It means your path is neutral.
3
Stock draw with neutral path (15 min): Now close the stance very slightly (feet 3-5 yards right). Swing along your foot line. Hit 15 balls. This should produce your goal: a controlled 3-5 yard draw with a moderate in-to-out path, not the exaggerated version you've been fighting.

Day 6 — Early Extension Fix

30 min | Home or Range ▼

Goal: Train your hips to rotate, not thrust. This is the physical foundation for consistent face control.

1
Wall/chair drill (10 min): Set up with your backside lightly touching a wall or chair. Make slow-motion swings. Your glutes must maintain contact through impact. If your hips thrust toward the ball, you lose contact. This is the feel of proper rotation without early extension. 20 reps.
2
Hip rotation isolation (10 min): Cross your arms over your chest, take your address position. Rotate your hips as if making a downswing — the lead hip should clear back and behind you, not slide toward the target. Do 20 reps, holding the "impact" position for 3 seconds each time. Your belt buckle should face the target while your hips stay back.
3
If at the range: Hit 15 balls with a 7-iron, at 70% speed, focusing solely on keeping your hips back while rotating through. Accept bad contact — you're training a motor pattern.

Day 7 — Driver Day

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Apply the path and rotation fixes to the driver. The driver amplifies every path error — if you can control it here, you own it everywhere.

1
Warm up with 7-iron (10 min): Feet-together drill (10 balls), then full swings with fade intent (5 balls), then stock draw (5 balls).
2
Driver — 3/4 speed only (15 min): Tee the ball up. Hit 15 drivers at 75% speed. Focus on: wider takeaway, rotation through impact, and the feel of swinging slightly "left" through the ball. Don't chase distance. A straight 230 beats a hooked 270 into the trees every time.
3
Driver — fade attempts (10 min): Try to hit 10 gentle fades with the driver. Open stance slightly, swing along your feet. If you can fade a driver, you have path control. If every fade attempt still draws or hooks, your path is still too inside — go back to the gate drill.
4
Driver — stock shot (10 min): Hit 10 drivers with your normal setup, trying for a 3-5 yard draw at 85% speed. Tally: push / hook / good / fade.

Day 8 — Integration & Iron Consistency

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Combine all Week 1-2 drills into a flowing practice. Work through the bag.

1
Warm up progression (10 min): Takeaway checkpoints (5 reps) → feet-together (5 balls) → half-swing fades (5 balls).
2
Club ladder (20 min): Hit 3 balls each with: PW, 8-iron, 6-iron, 4-iron/hybrid, driver. Same feel for each: wide takeaway, rotate through, gentle draw. Note which clubs still produce the two-way miss. Longer clubs will be harder — that's expected.
3
9-ball grid (15 min): Place 9 balls in a 3×3 grid. Row 1: fade. Row 2: straight. Row 3: draw. Use 7-iron. Work through all 9, pausing between each to reset. This is the single best drill for building face-to-path awareness.

Week 3 — Grooving the Stock Shot

The new path should be starting to feel less foreign. This week is about repetition, building trust, and extending the changes to all clubs. We also introduce on-course thinking.

Day 9 — Stock Draw Factory

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Hammer the stock 3-5 yard draw with a 7-iron until it's automatic.

1
Warm up (10 min): Feet-together (10 balls), focusing on clean contact and rotation.
2
Stock draw — 50 balls (30 min): Pick a specific target (a flag, a yardage sign). Set up with your alignment stick on the ground. Every ball gets the same routine: look at target, one practice swing feeling the wide takeaway, address, swing with rotation. After every 10 balls, tally push/hook/good. You should see the "good" percentage climbing.
3
Compare to baseline (5 min): Look at your Day 1 tally vs. today. Write down the numbers.
Benchmark

By Day 9, you should be hitting 35-40% good shots (up from 20%). If you're still at 20%, revisit the gate drill and fade practice from Week 2 — your path hasn't moved enough yet.

Day 10 — Full Bag Rotation

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Apply the stock draw to every club. The feel should be the same — only ball position and swing length change.

1
Warm up (10 min): Gate drill (10 slow swings) → full speed 7-iron draws (5 balls).
2
Ascending ladder (20 min): Hit 5 balls each: SW, PW, 8i, 6i, 4i/hybrid, 3-wood, driver. Same setup routine, same feels. After each club, note if the miss pattern changes. Common: the miss gets worse as clubs get longer. If your 6-iron and above are still mostly push/hook, spend more time on the fade drill with those clubs specifically.
3
Simulated holes (15 min): Pick 5 targets at different distances. For each one: choose the right club, commit to one target, hit one ball. Don't hit a second — just like on the course, you get one shot. Note the result and move on. This trains commitment, which is critical for eliminating the two-way miss.

Day 11 — Driver Focus + Speed

40 min | Range ▼

Goal: Start adding speed back to the driver while maintaining the path fix. The real test of any swing change is whether it holds under full speed.

1
Warm up (10 min): 7-iron stock draws (10 balls). Should be mostly automatic by now.
2
Driver speed ladder (15 min): Hit 5 balls at 60% speed, 5 at 75%, 5 at 85%, 5 at 95%. Same wide takeaway, same rotation. Monitor: at what speed does the old pattern reappear? That's your current ceiling. Practice at the speed just below that ceiling for the next 10 balls.
3
Driver commit drill (15 min): Pick a fairway-width target. Hit 10 drivers with full pre-shot routine, full commitment, at 85-90% speed. Play the draw — aim right edge of your target, expect 3-5 yards of draw. Tally fairways hit vs. missed, and which side you missed on.

Day 12 — Pressure Practice

30 min | Range ▼

Goal: Simulate course pressure. Changes that work on the range but collapse under pressure aren't real changes yet.

1
Play 9 holes on the range (30 min): Pick a course you know (or invent one). For each "hole," choose a club, pick a target, go through your full routine, and hit ONE ball. Score it honestly: fairway or miss, green or miss. Don't re-hit. Alternate between driver, long iron, mid iron, wedge to simulate a real round. Write your score out of 9.
Mental Key

The two-way miss feeds on indecision. When you stand over a ball worried about both the hook AND the push, you freeze. Pick your stock draw, aim for it, and commit. A committed swing to the wrong target is better than a tentative swing to the right one. You cannot steer the ball — you can only trust your preparation.

Week 4 — Course Transfer & Ownership

The mechanical work is done. This week is about transferring range gains to the course, building a pre-shot routine that locks in the feels, and playing with your new stock shot under real conditions.

Day 13 — Pre-Shot Routine Build

30 min | Range ▼

Goal: Build and lock in a repeatable pre-shot routine that embeds your swing keys.

1
Design your routine (10 min): Stand behind the ball. Pick your target line. Take one practice swing with your ONE swing thought (pick the one that helps most: "wide takeaway" or "rotate through" — not both). Address the ball. One look at the target. Go. The whole routine should take 15-20 seconds. Practice it 10 times without hitting a ball.
2
Routine + ball (20 min): Hit 30 balls. Every single one gets the full routine. No rapid-fire range balls — that builds bad habits. If you catch yourself skipping the routine, stop, step back, and restart.
One Swing Thought Maximum

On the course, you get ONE swing thought. Not two. Not three. Pick the one that's working best: "wide and rotate" or "swing through to the target" or whatever cue gives you the most consistent result. Everything else is range-only drill work.

Day 14 — Scoring Clubs Focus

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Dial in the clubs that matter most for scoring: wedges through 7-iron. These are your green-hitting clubs. To shoot in the 70s, you need to hit 11-12 greens per round.

1
Distance control ladder (20 min): With your PW, hit 5 balls each at: full, 3/4, 1/2 distance. Then 8-iron: full and 3/4. Then 6-iron: full. Observe your dispersion. The goal isn't just direction (draw vs. push) — it's also distance consistency. Note your landing zone spread for each club/distance.
2
Pin-hunter drill (15 min): Pick a flag or target between 100-160 yards. Hit 10 balls at it, alternating clubs. Full PW, then 3/4 9-iron, then full 8-iron, then 3/4 7-iron. Same target, different clubs. This trains commitment to a number and builds scoring-range confidence.
3
5-ball challenge (10 min): Pick a green-sized target (30 yards wide). Hit 5 balls with your most troublesome iron. How many land on the "green"? This is your greens-in-regulation percentage with that club. Repeat with your second most troublesome iron.

Day 15 — Full Simulation Round

45 min | Range ▼

Goal: Play a full simulated 18 holes on the range. This is your final range test before taking it to the course.

1
18-hole simulation (40 min): Pick a course layout from memory. For each hole: choose club off the tee, hit it. Based on result, choose approach club, hit it. Score each hole generously (fairway = good drive, on "green" = good approach). Full pre-shot routine on every single shot. Write your score.
2
Review (5 min): Count your misses. How many push-rights? How many pull-hooks? How many good shots? Compare to Day 1. This is your 30-day progress report on the range.

Day 16 — Take It to the Course

Round Day ▼

Goal: Play a round with ONE swing thought and your pre-shot routine. This is validation day.

1
Pre-round warm-up (15 min): Feet-together drill (10 balls). Stock draws (10 balls). 5 drivers. No more — save the good feels for the course.
2
On-course rules:
  • ONE swing thought only — the one from Day 13
  • Full pre-shot routine on every full swing
  • Play your stock draw on every shot — no steering, no manipulation
  • Accept results. Bad shots will happen. Don't change your approach mid-round.
  • Track: fairways hit, greens in regulation, and which side you missed on
3
Post-round review: Write down FIR, GIR, total score, and miss pattern. This is your course baseline with the new swing.
Expectation Setting

Your first round with new mechanics will likely NOT be your best score. Scores often dip before they improve because you're fighting old habits under pressure. Trust that the range work is real. The scoring improvement comes after the mechanics become subconscious — usually rounds 3-5 with the new pattern.

Drill Library

Quick reference for every drill in the plan. Bookmark this section for range sessions.

Path Fix

Alignment Stick Gate Drill

What it fixes: Excessively in-to-out path (the root cause).

Setup: Push two alignment sticks into the ground (or lean them against range baskets) about 8 inches apart, creating a gate just outside and in front of the ball, angled along your target line.

Execution: Swing through the gate. If you hit the outside stick, your path is too far inside. If you hit the inside stick, you've come over the top. Start with half-swings.

Reps: 10-15 minutes, 3x per week minimum during Weeks 1-2.

Path Fix

Intentional Fade Practice

What it fixes: Proves you can swing on a neutral or outside-in path. If you can't hit a fade, your path is still too far inside.

Setup: Open your stance 10-15 yards left of target. Ball position slightly forward of center.

Execution: Swing along your foot line (not at the target). Feel the club exit left after impact. The ball should start slightly left and curve gently right.

Key insight: If your "fades" still draw or go straight, you're making progress — your path is moving toward neutral. If they still hook, you need more time with the gate drill.

Rotation

Feet-Together Drill

What it fixes: Sway, slide, early extension, getting stuck. Forces pure rotation.

Setup: Stand with feet touching, 7-iron.

Execution: Make 3/4 swings. You'll feel your body must rotate to generate any power — you can't sway or slide. This automatically produces a more neutral path.

Reps: 10-15 balls. Use as a warm-up drill for every session.

Rotation

Wall/Chair Early Extension Drill

What it fixes: Hips thrusting toward the ball instead of rotating.

Setup: Address position with your backside lightly touching a wall, chair back, or golf cart.

Execution: Make slow-motion swings, maintaining glute contact through the "impact" zone. If your hips thrust forward, you'll feel yourself disconnect from the wall. That disconnect is early extension.

Reps: 20 slow-motion reps. Can be done at home without a club.

Takeaway

Takeaway Checkpoint Drill

What it fixes: Inside takeaway that sets up the stuck position.

Setup: 7-iron, normal address.

Execution: Take the club back slowly and stop when the shaft is parallel to the ground. Check two things: (1) the clubhead is outside your hands (not behind them), and (2) the toe of the club points roughly at the sky. If the clubhead is behind your hands with the face pointing at the ground, your takeaway is too inside and too shut.

Reps: 20-30 slow reps. Use as a range warm-up.

Awareness

9-Ball Grid Drill

What it fixes: Builds conscious control of face-to-path relationship.

Setup: Place 9 balls in a 3×3 grid. Assign shot shapes: Row 1 = fade, Row 2 = straight, Row 3 = draw.

Execution: Work through all 9, pausing between each ball to reset your setup for the intended shape. Use 7-iron. The goal is intentionality — knowing what you're doing with path and face, not just hoping.

Reps: Once through the grid = one set. Do 2-3 sets per session when used.

Connection

Glove-Under-Arm Drill

What it fixes: Arms disconnecting from body, getting stuck behind you.

Setup: Place a glove or small towel under your trail (right) armpit.

Execution: Make 3/4 swings keeping the glove in place through impact. If your trail arm separates from your body too early, the glove drops. This keeps the arms connected and prevents the stuck position.

Reps: 15-20 balls with 7-iron. Good warm-up drill.

Film Your Swing

Getting swing video is the single most valuable thing you can do without a launch monitor. Here's exactly how to set it up.

📱➡️

Down-the-Line (DTL)

  • Stand directly behind the ball, on the target line extended
  • Camera at hand height (not ground level, not head height)
  • Distance: far enough to see the full swing arc with the club visible at the top of the backswing
  • Aim camera at your hands at address
  • Use slow-motion (240fps on iPhone) if available

What it shows: Swing plane, path (inside/outside), takeaway direction, whether you're stuck, early extension

📱⬆️

Face-On (FO)

  • Stand directly in front of the golfer, perpendicular to the target line
  • Camera at belt/hip height
  • Center the frame on the ball position
  • Distance: full body in frame with room above for the club at the top
  • Use slow-motion if available

What it shows: Weight shift, hip sway vs. rotation, hand position at impact, follow-through, balance

Filming Tips

Use a tripod or phone mount — hand-held video is nearly useless for swing analysis.

Film 5 swings from each angle with the same club (7-iron is ideal).

Use slow-motion mode — 240fps on iPhone (Settings → Camera → Record Slo-mo). This lets you scrub through impact frame-by-frame.

Film in good light — avoid backlighting (don't face the sun).

Include the ball flight — let the camera run until the ball lands. Ball flight tells the story.

Don't "perform" — hit normal shots. The point is to capture your real swing, not your best swing.

What to Look For

When you review your video, check these specific things:

  • DTL — Takeaway: Does the clubhead drag inside immediately? It should track along or slightly inside the target line for the first foot.
  • DTL — Top of backswing: Where is the club shaft pointing? If it points right of target (across the line), you're set up to get stuck.
  • DTL — Downswing: Does the club drop behind your body? It should slot onto or just under the backswing plane.
  • FO — Impact: Have your hips rotated open, or are they still square/thrusting toward the ball?
  • FO — Follow-through: Is your chest facing the target at finish? Or are you hanging back with a stalled turn?

Course Strategy for the 70s

Beyond ball striking — the scoring framework to break 80 consistently.

The Math

Shooting 79 or Better — What It Takes

To shoot in the 70s, you need roughly:

  • 8-10 fairways (57-71%) — eliminating the two-way miss gets you here
  • 10-12 greens in regulation — ball striking consistency from 150 yards in
  • 30-32 putts — no three-putts, make a few 6-10 footers
  • No more than 1 double bogey — course management, not heroics
  • 2-4 birdies — capitalize on short approach shots

Tee Shot Strategy — Eliminate One Side

Once you have a stock 3-5 yard draw, your strategy changes fundamentally:

  • Aim at the right edge of the fairway. Your draw brings it back to center. If it goes straight (no draw), you're still in the right side of the fairway. The left side of the course is eliminated.
  • On tight holes, take 3-wood or hybrid off the tee. A straight 220 in the fairway beats a 270 draw-or-hook into trouble. From the high 80s to the 70s, the biggest scoring gains come from avoiding big numbers, not adding distance.
  • Know your miss. As your path neutralizes, your miss should become predominantly a slight push (face stays open to path). That's a manageable, one-directional miss. Play for it.

Approach Shot Strategy — Aim for the Fat of the Green

  • Stop firing at pins. From 150+ yards, aim at the center of the green. GIR percentage matters more than proximity. A 30-foot putt from the green is better than a chip from a bunker.
  • Know your distances. Spend 10 minutes on the range hitting each iron 10 times and noting your average carry. Most amateurs overestimate their distances by 5-10 yards, which means consistently coming up short.
  • When in doubt, take one more club. The front bunker or hazard catches more balls than the back of the green. Being 15 feet past the pin is better than being in a bunker 10 yards short.

The "Bogey is OK" Mindset

From the high 80s to the 70s, the biggest shift is mental:

  • Bogey is not a failure. You can shoot 79 with 7 bogeys and 4 birdies. Stop compounding bad holes by trying hero recovery shots.
  • After a bad tee shot: Punch out to the fairway. Wedge on. Two-putt. Walk away with bogey. That's a 5 on a par 4 — not a 7 after trying to thread a gap in the trees.
  • Par 5s are your scoring holes. Two good shots and a wedge to the green. These are where your birdies come from — not from aggressive driver plays on tight par 4s.

Recommended Training Aids

  • Alignment sticks (essential): $10-15. You need two minimum. Used for gate drill, alignment check, and path feedback. Non-negotiable.
  • Phone tripod/mount (essential): $15-20. For filming your swing. A suction-cup mount on a range ball basket works too.
  • Tour Striker Smart Ball ($30): Inflatable ball held between forearms. Prevents arms from separating and getting stuck. Good for home practice.
  • Basic launch monitor (optional but valuable): Garmin R10 (~$400) or Rapsodo MLM2PRO (~$500) would give you actual path and face numbers. Not required, but accelerates the feedback loop significantly.
